Framework Two parallel 1–10 scores — Asset and Operations — blended 60/40, with hard gates that kill a deal regardless of price and a recommended cap band that says what to actually pay. A distinct Stewardship & Regulatory overlay screens deals whose returns depend on the extractive rent-gouge playbook — the pattern that invites rent control, good-cause eviction laws, litigation and exit impairment. The design mirrors our DFW Retail buy box, re-tooled around the way manufactured-housing deals are actually won and lost.

Manufactured-housing communities and RV parks are the lowest cost-per-unit asset class in real estate — but they are an infrastructure business first. Sewer and water surprises, not tenant credit, are where deals get burned. This framework grades every candidate on the two levers that matter: the physical asset (utilities, market, scale, land) and the operating income (home-ownership mix, occupancy, lot-rent-to-market, expenses, bill-back). Hard gates reject lagoon/plant sewer with no city path, floodway sites, non-conforming zoning, predominantly park-owned inventory, and any deal whose only path to yield is rent hikes the law or the market won't bear. Each park earns a deterministic recommended cap band and a GO / CAUTION / NO-GO call.

The governing thesis (Chandler, Jul 2026) builds the platform on manufactured-housing communities as the defensive core and hybrid RV parks as a selective return sleeve — a 70% MHC / 30% RV equity allocation. The MHC case is structural: Sun Communities reported 97.7% same-property MH occupancy, only 0.3% annual physical move-outs, and 6.4% average 10-year same-property NOI growth; PGIM and IPA attribute the durability to constrained new supply (5.2% national vacancy) and residents who can't cheaply move a home. The RV case is real but cyclical in 2026 — RVIA cut shipments 8.2%, Campspot shows transient bookings down 2.2%, and ELS reported annual RV revenue up 4.1% while transient fell 8.5% — so the mandate is to buy hybrid parks with a monthly/annual recurring floor, not commodity transient parks priced on peak season. Texas adds a structural edge: it preempts municipal rent control. The buy box below is calibrated to this thesis — scale bands, DSCR floors, home-mix, wastewater verification and demand diversification are all scored against it.

The Two-Track Buy Box

§01 · Parallel Acquisition Frameworks

Two fundamentally different assets share this platform. Track A — Manufactured Housing Communities trades on land-lease durability: residents own their homes, turnover is near-zero because a move costs $5,000–$10,000, and value is created through utilities, infill, and gradual rent-to-market. Track B — RV Parks & Resorts trades on hospitality dynamics: nightly/monthly mix, seasonality, amenities, and destination pull. A deal only needs to win one track.

Track A · The Land-Lease Strategy

Manufactured Housing Communities

"Own the dirt, lease the pad, let the resident own the home."

Physical Asset

  • City water & city sewer — the gold standard; septic manageable, lagoon/package plants avoided absent a city-hookup path.
  • 50+ lots for financeability & on-site management economics; sub-25 is a structural kill.
  • Individual or sub-metered utilities so water/sewer bills back to residents ($30–$60/lot recovered).
  • Public roads, no floodway, conforming zoning; Sch-40 PVC / clay / cast-iron lines, not galvanized/polybutylene.

Operating Income

  • Primarily tenant-owned homes (POH < 40%); park-owned inventory adds capex & liability ($40k–$80k/home to replace).
  • Lot rent 10–30% below market — the value-add lever, closed gradually and sustainably.
  • 85–95% occupancy, trend flat-to-rising; expense ratio 35–45%.
  • Within ~1 hr of a 100k+ metro with stable-to-growing employment.
Track B · The Hospitality Strategy

RV Parks & Resorts

"Where destination pull, amenities and seasonality drive the pad."

Physical & Location

  • Destination or path-of-travel — tourism draw, lake/hill-country, or long-stay near employment/energy corridors.
  • Full hookups (W/S/E), pull-throughs; city utilities strongly preferred, amenities that support ADR.
  • No floodway, conforming zoning, room to add sites or annual/seasonal cabins.

Income Mix

  • Annual/monthly base + transient upside — annual leases smooth the seasonal curve.
  • Watch seasonality & concentration; underwrite off-season, not peak.
  • Amenity & ADR upside as the value-creation lever rather than pure rate hikes.
  • Occupancy read as economic, not just physical; verify against deposits.

Hard Gates · A Deal Fails These Regardless of Score
  • ∅ Lagoon or package-plant sewer with no path to city — the single biggest capex/regulatory liability in the asset class
  • ∅ Water source or sewer type unknown — missing infrastructure data reads as risk, not average
  • ∅ FEMA floodway, or non-conforming / unknown zoning (forced-closure risk)
  • ∅ Predominantly park-owned homes (>70%) — management & capex intensity of a housing operator, not a land-lease
  • ∅ Master-metered, park-paid utilities with no sub-meter path — margin exposed to every rate increase
  • ∅ Sub-scale (<20 pads) — fails financeability & on-site management economics
  • ∅ Rent-capped jurisdiction where the underwriting relies on hikes the law blocks — the extraction thesis is legally foreclosed
Stewardship & Regulatory Overlay · Why We Screen the Extraction Playbook
The private-equity model documented across the sector — buy, spike lot rents 40–60% (or double them), sell in ~4 years — works precisely because residents can't move a home for $5k–$10k. That "hostage" dependence is also the source of the risk: it has drawn rent-control and good-cause-eviction laws, class-action settlements over utilities and maintenance, Fannie/Freddie tenant-protection mandates, and national press that impairs exits. Our overlay flags any deal whose return depends on shock rent increases in a protected or protection-trending market, and rewards durable, sub-market-to-market business plans a responsible operator can defend. Lower regulatory beta is not just ethics — it is a cleaner, more financeable, more exit-ready asset.

Texas Market Filters & 2026 Cyclical Posture
RGV / South Texas: workforce, cross-border, Winter Texan demand — require flood, drainage & heat discipline. San Antonio / I-35 outer ring: employment corridors, don't overpay for growth narratives. DFW secondary/tertiary: favor industrial, logistics, military, medical over destination-dependent RV. Gulf Coast / Hill Country: RV only with documented elevation, flood history, evacuation & insurable BI. Industrial nodes: extended-stay demand can be strong but underwrite employer concentration & project end-dates.  Because 2026 transient RV demand is softening, RV underwriting leans on the recurring monthly/annual floor, not peak season.

Methodology

Two 1–10 scores (Asset, Operations) blended 60/40 into a composite that drives tier and a clamped recommended cap band (5.75%–8.75%). Fair value = disclosed NOI ÷ recommended cap where NOI is given, else price × asking cap. Structural gates are binary and independent of price; unknown critical inputs read as risk. A Stewardship & Regulatory overlay can gate an extraction-dependent deal in a rent-capped market and otherwise cap a decision at CAUTION.

Thesis-calibrated. Scale bands (MHC 75–300 / RV 80–250), the 60%/40% occupancy floors, ≥75% resident-owned preference, DSCR floors (1.35x MHC / 1.40x RV), wastewater verification, and demand diversification are all scored against the Chandler Texas MHC/RV thesis (Jul 2026). Each deal card carries a non-gating Thesis Fit read; the live allocation tracker measures the pipeline against the 70/30 mandate.
